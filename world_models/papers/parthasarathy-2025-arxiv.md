# Closing the Train-Test Gap in World Models for Gradient-Based Planning

> **One sentence.** Two data synthesis techniques -- Online World Modeling and Adversarial World Modeling -- close the train-test gap in latent world models, enabling gradient-based planning (GBP) to match or exceed the performance of search-based CEM at 10x less computation on robotic manipulation and navigation tasks.

**Authors:** Arjun Parthasarathy, Nimit Kalra, Rohun Agrawal, Yann LeCun, Oumayma Bounou, Pavel Izmailov, Micah Goldblum | **Venue:** arXiv 2025 | **arXiv:** [2512.09929](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.09929)

---

## Problem & Motivation

World models trained on next-state prediction objectives are used at test time to optimize sequences of actions via planning. This creates a fundamental train-test gap: during training the model sees single-step expert transitions, but during planning it must accurately predict multi-step rollouts under action sequences generated by gradient descent. Gradient-based planning (GBP) drives the world model into out-of-distribution latent states where prediction errors compound, and the optimization landscape induced by the world model may contain poor local minima and flat regions. As a result, GBP has consistently underperformed search-based methods like the Cross-Entropy Method (CEM), despite being orders of magnitude faster. Prior work has not addressed this gap at the world model training level.

---

## Core Idea

The authors identify two root causes for GBP failure: (1) gradient descent on actions pushes the world model into unfamiliar latent regions where predictions are unreliable, and (2) the loss surface over actions is non-smooth, hindering optimization. They propose two complementary finetuning strategies. Online World Modeling uses the simulator to correct GBP-generated trajectories, expanding the world model's reliable prediction region through a DAgger-style loop. Adversarial World Modeling perturbs expert states and actions in the direction that maximally increases prediction error (FGSM-style), forcing the model to be robust near expert trajectories and smoothing the planning loss landscape -- without requiring simulator access.

---

## How It Works

### Overview

A latent world model f_theta maps (z_t, a_t) -> z_{t+1} in a frozen DINOv2 embedding space. GBP optimizes action sequences by backpropagating through the differentiable rollout to minimize distance to a goal state. The two proposed finetuning methods modify the training data distribution to make the world model more suitable for GBP.

### Latent World Model

- **Encoder:** Frozen DINOv2 pretrained ViT maps observations o_t to latent states z_t = Phi(o_t)
- **Dynamics model:** MLP f_theta predicts next latent state z_{t+1} = f_theta(z_t, a_t)
- **Training objective:** L2 teacher-forcing loss: min_theta E[||f_theta(Phi(o_t), a_t) - Phi(o_{t+1})||^2]
- **Planning (GBP):** Initialize actions from N(0, I), optimize via gradient descent on ||z_hat_{H+1} - z_goal||^2 using a weighted goal loss (gradient from each predicted state, not just the final one)
- **MPC:** Re-plan at each step, executing only the first K <= H actions before replanning

### Online World Modeling

A DAgger-inspired approach requiring simulator access:
1. Sample an expert trajectory, run GBP to get planned actions
2. Execute those actions in the true simulator to get corrected states
3. Build corrected trajectory tau' = (z_1, a_hat_1, z'_2, ..., z'_{H+1})
4. Finetune the world model on these corrected trajectories
5. Repeat for N iterations

This expands the world model's reliable prediction region to cover states that GBP actually visits during planning.

### Adversarial World Modeling

Does not require simulator access -- works on offline data only:
1. Sample a minibatch of (z_t, a_t, z_{t+1}) transitions
2. Compute perturbation radii epsilon_a, epsilon_z proportional to the standard deviation of actions and states (scaled by lambda_a, lambda_z)
3. Apply FGSM-style perturbations: signed gradient ascent on the prediction loss to find worst-case delta_a, delta_z within the perturbation budget
4. Finetune the world model on the adversarially perturbed trajectories

The minimax objective: min_theta E[max_{delta_a, delta_z} ||f_theta(Phi(o_t) + delta_z, a_t + delta_a) - Phi(o_{t+1})||^2]

### Training

- **Base world model:** DINO-WM (Zhou et al., 2025) with DINOv2 encoder (frozen) and ViT transition model
- **Tasks:** PushT (2D object manipulation), PointMaze (2D navigation), Wall (navigation with obstacles)
- **Optimizer:** Adam used during GBP; random initialization outperforms learned initialization networks
- **Also validated on IRIS world model architecture** (see Appendix B.3)

### Inference

At test time, GBP runs Algorithm 1: initialize random actions, iteratively backpropagate through the world model rollout to minimize goal distance, then execute via MPC. Adversarial World Modeling achieves this at 10x less wall-clock time than CEM.

## Results

### Planning Performance (Success Rate %)

| Model | PushT (Adam) | PushT (CEM) | PointMaze (Adam) | PointMaze (CEM) | Wall (Adam) | Wall (CEM) |
|-------|-------------|-------------|-----------------|----------------|------------|------------|
| DINO-WM + MPC | 76 | 92 | 68 | 90 | 80 | 82 |
| Online WM + MPC | 76 | 92 | 88 | 96 | 80 | 90 |
| **Adversarial WM + MPC** | **92** | **92** | **94** | **98** | **94** | **94** |

Adversarial World Modeling with Adam GBP matches or exceeds CEM on all three tasks. In the open-loop setting, GBP gains are +18% on PushT, +20% on PointMaze, and +30% on Wall.

### Train-Test Gap

Figure 4 shows that DINO-WM has substantially higher prediction error on planning trajectories than on expert trajectories. Both Online WM and Adversarial WM close this gap, with prediction errors on planning trajectories comparable to those on expert data.

### Computational Efficiency

GBP with Adversarial WM achieves near-CEM performance at over 10x less wall-clock time. On PushT, GBP reaches peak performance in ~10 minutes while CEM requires ~500 minutes (Figure 3).

### Loss Landscape Smoothing

Figure 2 visualizes the optimization landscape before and after adversarial finetuning. The adversarial model produces a smoother surface with a broader basin around the optimum, directly explaining why gradient-based optimization succeeds.

## Comparison to Prior Work

| | This Paper | CEM (search-based) | DreamerV3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Planning method | Gradient-based (backprop through world model) | Sampling-based (iterative population search) | Actor-critic (latent imagination) |
| Compute at inference | Low (single gradient pass) | High (many rollout evaluations) | Low (forward pass) |
| Simulator needed? | Only for Online WM; not for Adversarial WM | No | No |
| Train-test gap addressed | Yes (core contribution) | N/A (no learned optimization) | No |

**vs [[lecun-2022-openreview]] ([H-JEPA](../papers/lecun-2022-openreview.md)):** LeCun's position paper envisions gradient-based planning through differentiable world models as a core component of autonomous intelligence. This paper directly addresses the practical challenge that makes GBP unreliable -- the train-test distribution gap -- providing concrete algorithms that realize the vision of efficient gradient-based planning.

**vs [[hafner-2023-arxiv]] ([DreamerV3](../papers/hafner-2023-arxiv.md)):** DreamerV3 sidesteps the planning optimization problem by training an actor-critic in latent imagination. This paper instead tackles planning directly via gradient-based optimization through the world model, showing it can match search-based alternatives with proper training. The two approaches are complementary -- actor-critic for speed, GBP for goal-conditioned flexibility.

**vs [[hafner-2019-icml]] ([PlaNet](../papers/hafner-2019-icml.md)):** PlaNet uses CEM for planning through a learned RSSM. This paper shows that with proper world model training, gradient-based planning can replace CEM while being 10x faster, on models built with modern pretrained visual encoders (DINOv2).

**vs [[maes-2026-arxiv]] ([LeWorldModel](../papers/maes-2026-arxiv.md)):** LeWorldModel also performs latent planning with CEM through a JEPA-style world model. This work demonstrates that gradient-based planning can serve as a viable alternative to CEM by closing the train-test gap, which could benefit LeWorldModel-style architectures as well.

## Strengths

- **Addresses a real, underappreciated gap:** The train-test mismatch between next-state prediction training and multi-step planning is clearly identified and empirically demonstrated
- **Two complementary solutions:** Online WM (with simulator) and Adversarial WM (offline only) cover different practical scenarios
- **Strong empirical gains:** Adversarial WM enables GBP to match CEM at 10x less compute -- a practical, not just theoretical, improvement
- **Clear visualization of mechanism:** Loss landscape plots (Figure 2) directly show how adversarial training smooths the optimization surface
- **Broadly applicable:** Validated on both DINO-WM and IRIS architectures; the techniques are model-agnostic

---

## Weaknesses & Limitations

- **Limited task complexity:** Evaluated only on 2D robotic tasks (PushT, PointMaze, Wall) -- not tested on high-dimensional manipulation, 3D environments, or Atari
- **Online World Modeling requires a simulator:** The DAgger-style approach assumes access to a ground-truth simulator for trajectory correction, limiting applicability to sim-only settings
- **Single-step perturbation only:** Adversarial WM uses FGSM (single gradient step), not multi-step PGD attacks -- the authors note comparable results but stronger adversaries might yield further gains
- **No comparison to actor-critic methods:** The paper compares GBP to CEM but does not benchmark against DreamerV3 or TD-MPC style actor-critic planning on the same tasks
- **Frozen encoder assumption:** The approach relies on a pretrained DINOv2 encoder; it is unclear how the techniques would interact with jointly trained encoders

---

## Key Takeaways

- World models trained on next-state prediction suffer a fundamental train-test gap when used for gradient-based planning: GBP drives the model into out-of-distribution states where prediction errors compound and the loss landscape is non-smooth
- Adversarial World Modeling -- finetuning the world model on FGSM-perturbed states and actions -- closes this gap without requiring simulator access, smoothing the planning loss landscape and enabling GBP to match or exceed CEM
- Gradient-based planning with adversarially trained world models achieves 10x computational speedup over search-based CEM while matching success rates on robotic manipulation and navigation tasks
- The techniques are architecture-agnostic (validated on DINO-WM and IRIS) and could benefit any latent world model used for planning, including JEPA-based architectures like [[maes-2026-arxiv]] ([LeWorldModel](../papers/maes-2026-arxiv.md))
