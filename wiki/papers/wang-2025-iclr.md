# Drama: Mamba-Enabled Model-Based Reinforcement Learning Is Sample and Parameter Efficient

> **Drama** replaces the recurrent state-space model (RSSM) in the Dreamer framework with a Mamba-2 state space model, achieving competitive Atari 100k performance with only a 7-million-parameter world model -- roughly 30x smaller than DreamerV3 -- while introducing Dynamic Frequency-based Sampling (DFS) to mitigate early-training suboptimality from inaccurate world models.

**Authors:** Wenlong Wang, Ivana Dusparic, Yucheng Shi, Ke Zhang, Vinny Cahill | **Venue:** ICLR 2025 | **arXiv:** [2410.08893](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.08893)

---

## Problem & Motivation

Model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) improves sample efficiency by training policies on imagined trajectories generated by a learned world model, rather than relying solely on real environment interactions. However, the sequence models at the core of these world models face fundamental trade-offs. RNNs (including the GRU-based RSSM used in [[hafner-2023-arxiv]] ([DreamerV3](../papers/hafner-2023-arxiv.md))) suffer from vanishing gradients and limited long-term dependency capture. Transformers, used in [[micheli-2023-iclr]] ([IRIS](../papers/micheli-2023-iclr.md)) and [[robine-2023-iclr]] ([TWM](../papers/robine-2023-iclr.md)), have quadratic O(n^2) memory and computational complexity in sequence length, making long-sequence world modeling prohibitively expensive. A further challenge shared by all MBRL methods is that early in training, the world model is inaccurate, and policies trained on these incorrect imagined trajectories converge to suboptimal behaviour that is difficult to correct later.

Drama addresses both problems: it leverages the Mamba state space model to achieve O(n) complexity (enabling efficient long-sequence training), and introduces Dynamic Frequency-based Sampling (DFS) to prioritize transitions where the world model is well-calibrated during imagination.

---

## Core Idea

Drama is the first model-based RL agent to use the Mamba SSM (specifically Mamba-2) as the backbone of its world model. Mamba-2 reformulates state space dynamics as semi-separable matrix multiplications, bridging SSMs and causal linear attention while maintaining linear scaling in sequence length. This allows Drama to train on longer sequences than transformer-based world models without the quadratic memory cost, while also being dramatically more parameter-efficient: Drama's entire world model uses only 7M parameters (XS configuration) compared to DreamerV3's 200M. Additionally, DFS biases replay buffer sampling toward transitions that the world model has learned well but the behaviour policy has not yet exploited, reducing the damage caused by inaccurate early-training predictions.

## How It Works

### Overview

Drama follows the standard Dreamer-style MBRL loop: (1) collect real experience and store it in a replay buffer, (2) train a world model on sampled sequences, and (3) train a behaviour policy on imagined trajectories ("imagination") generated autoregressively by the world model. The world model consists of a discrete variational autoencoder (VAE), a Mamba-2 sequence model, and MLP heads for reward and termination prediction. The POMDP formulation is standard: at each time step t, the agent observes a high-dimensional image O_t, selects action a_t, receives reward r_t and termination flag e_t, and transitions to the next state.

### Discrete Variational Autoencoder

The autoencoder follows [[hafner-2021-iclr]] ([DreamerV2](../papers/hafner-2021-iclr.md)):
- **Encoder:** Maps raw observation O_t to a discrete latent vector z_t ~ p(z_t | O_t) using a fully-connected discretization layer.
- **Decoder:** Reconstructs the original frame from z_t (not from predicted embeddings z-hat_t).
- **Gradient flow:** Straight-through estimator (Bengio et al., 2013) bypasses the discrete sampling operation during backpropagation.

### Mamba-2 Sequence Model

At each time step, the current latent z_t and action a_t are concatenated, projected through a fully-connected layer, and fed as input x_t to the Mamba blocks. The input channel dimension is divided by the head dimension p to produce multi-head processing. The Mamba-2 block computes the deterministic recurrent state d_t as:

- Sequence model: d_t = f(z_{t-l:t}, a_{t-l:t}; omega)
- Latent predictor: z-hat_{t+1} ~ p(z-hat_{t+1} | d_t; omega)

Mamba-2 formulates the SSM as a semi-separable matrix multiplication M = L . CB^T, where L is a lower-triangular "attention-like" matrix with input-dependent scalar entries a_t in [0,1]. When all a_t = 1, this reduces to causal linear attention. The matrix can be decomposed into q x q sub-matrices for efficient GPU computation. This achieves 2-8x faster training than original Mamba while maintaining linear scaling.

**Critical design choice for parallelism:** Unlike DreamerV3 where z_{t+1} depends on both the observation O_{t+1} and the deterministic state d_t (creating a sequential dependency), Drama's latent target z_{t+1} depends solely on O_{t+1}. This eliminates sequential dependencies in the training process, enabling full GPU parallelism across the sequence dimension.

The deterministic state d_t is used to predict the next embedding z-hat_{t+1}, reward r-hat_t, and termination flag e-hat_t via separate MLP heads.

### Behaviour Policy Learning

The behaviour policy is an actor-critic trained within "imagination":
1. Sample a batch of b_img trajectories of length l_img from the replay buffer.
2. Use real-world transitions to estimate an informative hidden state for the Mamba blocks (roll-outs begin from the last transition of each sequence at step l_img).
3. Continue for h imagination steps using autoregressively generated latent states.
4. The policy state concatenates the predicted discrete variable z-hat_t with the deterministic variable d_t.
5. Standard actor-critic with loss functions and value normalization from [[hafner-2023-arxiv]] ([DreamerV3](../papers/hafner-2023-arxiv.md)) and Andrychowicz et al. (2021).

**Key advantage during imagination:** Unlike transformers, Mamba updates its inference parameters independently of sequence length, accelerating the autoregressive imagination process. The hidden state automatically resets at episode boundaries (Mamba/Mamba-2 are input-dependent), unlike prior SSM meta-RL approaches (Lu et al., 2023) that require manual resets.

### Dynamic Frequency-Based Sampling (DFS)

DFS addresses the problem that early in training the world model underestimates rewards due to inaccuracy, causing the policy to converge to suboptimal behaviour. Two frequency vectors are maintained, each matching the replay buffer size:

- **v** (world model vector): v_i counts how many times transition i has been sampled for world model training.
- **b** (behaviour policy vector): b_i counts how many times transition i has been sampled for imagination.

Sampling probabilities for world model training: (p_1, ..., p_|E|) = softmax(-v), prioritizing under-trained transitions (similar to [[robine-2023-iclr]] ([TWM](../papers/robine-2023-iclr.md))).

Sampling probabilities for imagination: (p_1, ..., p_|E|) = softmax(f(v, b)), where f(v, b) = v - b - max(0, v - b). This creates two cases:
- If v_i >= b_i (well-trained by world model): f = 0, so the transition has baseline sampling probability.
- If v_i < b_i (under-trained or overfitted by policy): f = v_i - b_i < 0, reducing the sampling probability.

This ensures imagination preferentially uses transitions where the world model is reliable.

### Training Details

- **Benchmark:** Atari 100k (26 games), 100k environment steps (400k frames with 4-frame skipping).
- **World model parameters:** 7M (XS model), 10M (XS with DFS in main results).
- **Seeds:** 5 independent seeds per game, with 5-episode running average for evaluation.
- **Sequence length:** Longer than transformer-based approaches due to O(n) scaling.
- **Built on:** STORM infrastructure (Zhang et al., 2023).
- **Hardware:** Trainable on standard laptop hardware (off-the-shelf, 8GB GPU).

## Results

### Atari 100k Benchmark

| Method | Params | Norm. Mean (%) | Norm. Median (%) |
|--------|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| DreamerV3 | 200M | 125 | 49 |
| STORM | -- | 127 | 58 |
| **DramaXS** | **7M** | **105** | **27** |
| IRIS | -- | 96 | 51 |
| TWM | 21.6M | 62 | 40 |
| SPR | -- | 33 | 13 |
| SimPLe | -- | 11 | 3 |

Despite using only a 7M-parameter world model (the XS configuration), Drama achieves a normalised mean score of 105% -- competitive with IRIS (96%) and TWM (62%) and within striking distance of DreamerV3 (125%) and STORM (127%), which use dramatically larger models (DreamerV3's world model is ~200M parameters, 30x larger).

### Per-Game Highlights

- **Strong performance:** Boxing (78 vs. DreamerV3's 82), Pong (15 vs. 15), Asterix (1632 vs. 1062 -- Drama exceeds DreamerV3), Krull (9693 vs. 8171), MsPacman (2270 vs. 1521).
- **Weak performance:** Breakout (7 vs. DreamerV3's 10), PrivateEye (90 vs. 3238), Jamesbond (372 vs. 540). Struggles in sparse-reward games and games requiring a more robust autoencoder.
- **Excels in:** Games with clear opponent dynamics (Boxing, Pong), object tracking (Asterix), and long sequential memory (Krull, MsPacman).

### DramaXS vs. DreamerV3XS (Parameter-Matched Comparison)

When comparing Drama (10M params) against a 12M-parameter variant of DreamerV3 (DreamerV3XS), Drama significantly outperforms:

| Metric | DramaXS (10M) | DreamerV3XS (12M) |
|--------|--------------|-------------------|
| Norm. Mean (%) | 105 | 37 |
| Norm. Median (%) | 27 | 7 |

Drama outperforms DreamerV3XS in the vast majority of the 26 games, demonstrating that Mamba-2 is substantially more parameter-efficient than the GRU-based RSSM at small model scales.

### Ablation: DFS vs. Uniform Sampling

| Sampling | Norm. Mean (%) | Norm. Median (%) |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| DFS | 105 | 27 |
| Uniform | 80 | 28 |

DFS yields a 25-percentage-point improvement in normalised mean score over uniform sampling, while median performance is comparable. DFS shows particularly strong gains in games requiring adaptation to evolving dynamics (Alien, Asterix, BankHeist, Seaquest) and opponent-based games (Boxing, Pong). DFS underperforms in Breakout and KungFuMaster, where critical game dynamics are accessible early in gameplay.

### Ablation: Mamba vs. Mamba-2

In head-to-head comparison on Krull, Boxing, Freeway, and Kangaroo, Mamba-2 outperforms the original Mamba in 3 of 4 games. Mamba-2's structural restriction (diagonal matrix A forced to scalar multiples of identity) slightly reduces expressive power but significantly improves training efficiency through GPU-friendly matrix multiplication, resulting in a net positive trade-off for MBRL.

### Long-Sequence Grid World Experiment

A custom 5x5 grid world was used to compare long-sequence modeling capabilities:

| Method | Seq. Len. | Train Time (ms) | Memory (%) | Error (%) |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|------------|-----------|
| Mamba-2 | 208 | 25 | 13 | 15.6 +/- 2.6 |
| Mamba-2 | 1664 | 214 | 55 | 14.2 +/- 0.3 |
| Mamba | 208 | 34 | 14 | 13.9 +/- 0.4 |
| Mamba | 1664 | 299 | 52 | 14.0 +/- 0.4 |
| GRU | 208 | 75 | 66 | 21.3 +/- 0.3 |
| GRU | 1664 | 628 | 68 | 34.7 +/- 25.4 |
| Transformer | 208 | 45 | 17 | 24.7 +/- 7.4 |
| Transformer | 1664 | -- | OOM | -- |

Mamba and Mamba-2 achieve equivalent low error and fast training at both sequence lengths. The Transformer encounters out-of-memory (OOM) at l=1664 on an 8GB GPU. GRU degrades dramatically at long sequences (34.7% error with high variance). Mamba-2 achieves the lowest training time at both sequence lengths.

## Comparison to Prior Work

| Method | Sequence Model | Complexity | Params | Latent Space | Norm. Mean |
|--------|---------------|-----------|--------|-------------|-----------|
| **Drama** | Mamba-2 (SSM) | O(n) | 7M | Discrete (VAE) | 105% |
| DreamerV3 | GRU (RSSM) | O(n) | 200M | Continuous + categorical | 125% |
| IRIS | Autoregressive Transformer | O(n^2) | -- | Discrete (VQ-VAE) | 96% |
| TWM | Transformer-XL | O(n^2) | 21.6M | Discrete (VAE) | 62% |
| STORM | Stochastic Transformer | O(n^2) | -- | Discrete (VQ-VAE) | 127% |
| DIAMOND | Diffusion U-Net | -- | -- | Pixel (continuous) | -- |

**[[hafner-2023-arxiv]] ([DreamerV3, Hafner et al., 2023](../papers/hafner-2023-arxiv.md))** is the primary baseline. DreamerV3 uses a GRU-based RSSM with 200M parameters and achieves superhuman normalised mean on Atari 100k. Drama replaces the RSSM with Mamba-2, achieving competitive performance with 30x fewer parameters. However, Drama does not match DreamerV3's absolute performance, partly because DreamerV3 benefits from robustness techniques and a much larger autoencoder. In the parameter-matched comparison (DramaXS 10M vs. DreamerV3XS 12M), Drama wins decisively (105% vs. 37% mean).

**[[hafner-2021-iclr]] ([DreamerV2, Hafner et al., 2021](../papers/hafner-2021-iclr.md))** introduced the discrete latent representation and the RSSM architecture that Drama inherits for the VAE component. Drama's autoencoder follows DreamerV2's design, while replacing the recurrent dynamics backbone.

**[[micheli-2023-iclr]] ([IRIS, Micheli et al., 2023](../papers/micheli-2023-iclr.md))** uses a VQ-VAE tokenizer and an autoregressive GPT transformer for dynamics. IRIS operates on raw images for behaviour policy training (requiring image reconstruction during imagination), making it more computationally expensive. Drama achieves a higher normalised mean (105% vs. 96%) with a transparent parameter count of only 7M.

**[[robine-2023-iclr]] ([TWM, Robine et al., 2023](../papers/robine-2023-iclr.md))** uses a Transformer-XL as its dynamics model with a 21.6M-parameter world model. TWM's balanced sampling method (softmax(-v/tau)) directly inspired Drama's DFS approach for world model training, though Drama extends this idea to also handle imagination sampling via the dual-vector (v, b) mechanism. Drama substantially outperforms TWM (105% vs. 62% normalised mean).

**[[alonso-2024-neurips]] ([DIAMOND, Alonso et al., 2024](../papers/alonso-2024-neurips.md))** takes an orthogonal approach, using a diffusion model in pixel space as the world model. While DIAMOND achieves higher absolute performance on Atari 100k, it operates at a very different compute and complexity regime. Drama's SSM approach is complementary -- both demonstrate that alternatives to the dominant transformer/RSSM paradigm are viable for MBRL world models.

## Strengths
- First model-based RL agent using Mamba/Mamba-2 as the world model backbone, establishing that SSMs are a viable alternative to both RNNs and transformers for MBRL dynamics models.
- Extreme parameter efficiency: 7M-parameter world model achieves performance competitive with methods using 20-200M parameters, trainable on a standard laptop with an 8GB GPU.
- O(n) memory and computational complexity enables training on longer sequences than transformer-based world models, with empirical evidence from the grid world experiment that transformers run OOM at the same sequence lengths where Mamba remains efficient.
- DFS is a simple, principled sampling strategy that yields a 25-point normalised mean improvement over uniform sampling, addressing the well-known early-training suboptimality problem in MBRL.
- Thorough ablation study covering DFS vs. uniform sampling, Mamba vs. Mamba-2, and a long-sequence grid world comparison against GRU and Transformer baselines.
- Full parallelism during world model training (eliminating the sequential z_{t+1} dependency present in DreamerV3) is an elegant architectural simplification.

## Weaknesses & Limitations
- Absolute performance does not match DreamerV3 (125%) or STORM (127%) at full model scale; the paper primarily demonstrates efficiency rather than SOTA scores.
- Normalised median of 27% is notably lower than DreamerV3 (49%), IRIS (51%), and STORM (58%), indicating inconsistent per-game performance -- Drama achieves very high scores in some games but low scores in others.
- Evaluated only on discrete-action Atari 100k; no continuous-control experiments (DMControl, MuJoCo) or complex 3D environments (Minecraft, Habitat).
- The autoencoder is acknowledged as a bottleneck (e.g., poor Breakout performance), but no experiments explore scaling or improving it.
- DFS introduces two additional bookkeeping vectors and a custom sampling distribution; the paper does not analyze the computational overhead or sensitivity to buffer size.
- Sparse-reward games (Jamesbond, PrivateEye) remain challenging -- Mamba's long-range modeling advantages do not directly translate to reward signal discovery.
- The paper does not report wall-clock training times for the full Atari 100k experiments, making it hard to compare practical efficiency against baselines.

## Key Takeaways
- Mamba-2 is a viable and highly parameter-efficient replacement for both GRU-RSSM and transformer dynamics models in MBRL world models, achieving competitive Atari 100k performance with 30x fewer parameters than DreamerV3.
- At matched parameter counts (~10M), Mamba-2 dramatically outperforms GRU-based RSSM (Drama 105% vs. DreamerV3XS 37% normalised mean), suggesting SSMs scale down more gracefully.
- O(n) complexity is a practical advantage: Mamba and Mamba-2 handle sequence lengths of 1664 tokens within 8GB GPU memory, while transformers run OOM at the same length.
- Mamba-2's semi-separable matrix formulation is 2-8x faster than original Mamba for training, with a slight reduction in expressivity that is empirically beneficial for MBRL.
- Dynamic Frequency-based Sampling (DFS) is a simple but effective technique that biases imagination toward well-learned transitions, yielding 25% normalised mean improvement over uniform sampling.
- Drama demonstrates that accessible, laptop-trainable world models can approach the performance of large-scale MBRL systems, potentially democratizing world model research.
